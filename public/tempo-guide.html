Tempo

Gentle timers for movement, focus, and breath.

Tempo is a small, calm companion for the daily practices that keep body and mind well — a set on the mat, a focused work block, a few slow breaths before sleep. It doesn’t track you, score you, or nag. It just keeps time, quietly, so you can give your attention to the practice instead of the clock.

Everything in Tempo is the same underneath: a session is a list of timed steps, and the app counts you through them with a soft cue at each change. What differs is the shape of that list — and Tempo comes with five ready-made shapes plus a build-your-own. Pick one, adjust the numbers to suit you, and begin.

How it works

  • Choose a timer from the home screen. Each remembers the last settings you used, shown right on its card.
  • Set the numbers — lengths and counts — then tap Begin. A big ring counts down the current step, tinted to its kind, with the step name in the middle.
  • Cues. Movement and focus timers give a light ding at each change, with a quiet heads-up tick (and a matching glow on the ring) in the last few seconds so a change never catches you off guard; the three breathing/meditation practices use a soft, uninterrupted bell instead. Turn on the interval chime for longer sits and you'll hear a third, distinct sound every few minutes, so it never gets mistaken for a step change. On a phone that supports it, a gentle vibration comes along too. In Settings (the gear) you can set cue volume (Soft/Normal/Loud) — with a Preview button to hear it before you commit — choose how long the heads-up tick runs (3, 5, or 8 seconds), add a 3·2·1 count-in before a session starts, and choose the chime interval (3, 5, or 10 minutes). There's also a one-tap Silent mode that mutes every sound at once without losing your other settings — vibration keeps working, since that's not really a sound.
  • Controls. Pause/Resume, Skip, and Exit are on screen, sized to hit at a glance rather than aim for. Exit needs a second tap ("Tap again") within a couple of seconds, so a stray touch mid-movement can't end a session by accident. On a keyboard: Space pauses and resumes, → (or S) skips a step, Esc exits (same double-press rule), and Enter goes again when you finish.
  • It keeps your place. Close the app mid-session and it’ll be waiting, paused, right where you left it when you come back.
  • Make it yours. Tap the reorder icon (top right of the home screen) to rearrange the cards so your usual practice is first — it remembers the order. Switch between Light and Dark from the same row; this guide follows the same choice. Install Tempo to your home screen and it runs full-screen and offline.

The five practices

Each is a well-worn method from movement or contemplative traditions. Here’s the gist of each, and where it comes from.

Movement

Rounds

Alternating effort and rest, repeated — the backbone of interval training. The defaults (20 seconds on, 10 off, eight rounds) are a Tabata set, the protocol Dr. Izumi Tabata studied in the 1990s: short, hard efforts with brief recovery. But the same shape carries any interval work — a yoga flow held for time, calisthenics, mobility drills, a walk/jog. Add a warm-up if you like a moment to ease in.

Focus

Cycles

Focused stretches with real breaks between them. The defaults follow the Pomodoro Technique that Francesco Cirillo named in the late 1980s (after a tomato-shaped kitchen timer): work in ~25-minute bouts, take a short break, and a longer one every fourth cycle. Good for study, writing, or any deep work where the break matters as much as the push.

Meditation

Sit–Walk

A longer sitting practice, broken by slow, deliberate walking — the rhythm used in Vipassana retreats to rest the body and keep attention fresh across a long session. The default splits 45 minutes into 20 seated, 5 walking, 20 seated. When the bell moves you to Walk, rise and pace slowly and attentively; when it calls you back, settle in again. Add more seated blocks for a longer sit.

Breath

4-7-8 Breathing

A calming pranayama pattern popularised by Dr. Andrew Weil: breathe in through the nose for a count of four, hold for seven, and exhale slowly through the mouth for eight. The long exhale is the point — it’s what settles the body. Four breaths is the classic dose, often used to wind down or fall asleep. Let the bell mark each phase so you can keep your eyes closed.

Breath

Box Breathing

Four equal counts — in, hold, out, hold — like tracing the sides of a square. Widely taught for steadying the nervous system (it’s a staple of the calm-under-pressure toolkit used by athletes and first responders alike). Even and unhurried is the whole idea; four seconds a side is a gentle start, and you can lengthen each count as it becomes comfortable.

Your own

Custom

Build any sequence step by step — a label, a length, and what kind each step is (prepare, active, rest, or breathe). Reorder freely. It’s there for everything the presets don’t cover: a physio routine, a bedtime wind-down, a study ritual, a stretch series. Tempo remembers your last sequence and shows a summary of it on the home card.

A gentle word on habits

The practices here reward showing up more than pushing hard. A few slow breaths done daily will do more for you than a heroic session you dread and skip. Keep the numbers modest enough that beginning feels easy, let the cues carry the timing, and let the rest take care of itself. Tempo will keep the time; you keep the cadence.
