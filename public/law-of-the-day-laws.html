The laws

Law 1

↑ Top

Never Outshine the Master

When you work under someone more powerful, resist the urge to prove how clever or capable you are at their expense. Superiors want to feel secure in their own superiority; making them look slow or outdated in front of others tends to provoke resentment rather than admiration, even if your intentions were innocent. The safer path is to let your competence serve their status rather than compete with it.

Law 2

↑ Top

Never Put Too Much Trust in Friends, Learn How to Use Enemies

Friendships can carry hidden fault lines — envy, favoritism, and the discomfort of mixing business with affection — that make friends unreliable in high-stakes situations. A former rival, by contrast, often has more to prove and fewer illusions about the relationship, which can make them a more dependable ally once won over.

Law 3

↑ Top

Conceal Your Intentions

People defend against threats they can see coming. By keeping your real aims vague or hidden behind a plausible alternative motive, you deny others the chance to organize against you before you've acted.

Law 4

↑ Top

Always Say Less Than Necessary

Excess talk exposes your reasoning, your uncertainty, and your weaknesses. Brevity, by contrast, projects control and forces others to fill the silence — often revealing more of themselves in the process than you reveal of yourself.

Law 5

↑ Top

So Much Depends on Reputation — Guard It with Your Life

Reputation is a kind of leverage that operates even when you're not in the room. A strong one can substitute for actual force in a negotiation; a damaged one invites attacks from people who'd never have dared otherwise. Protecting it is worth real effort.

Law 6

↑ Top

Court Attention at All Costs

Being overlooked is its own kind of danger — obscurity means irrelevance. Cultivating visibility, distinctiveness, or even a bit of theater ensures you're accounted for in other people's decisions, which is where influence actually gets exercised.

Law 7

↑ Top

Get Others to Do the Work for You, but Always Take the Credit

Time and energy are finite; other people's skill and labor are a resource. The law isn't only about exploitation — it's about recognizing that the person who directs and synthesizes effort is often remembered as its author, for better or worse.

Law 8

↑ Top

Make Other People Come to You — Use Bait if Necessary

Whoever initiates an interaction on someone else's terms is already at a disadvantage. Creating a reason for others to approach you — an opportunity, a scarcity, a piece of leverage — keeps the terms of engagement in your hands.

Law 9

↑ Top

Win Through Your Actions, Never Through Argument

Verbal persuasion invites counter-argument and rarely overcomes deep-seated belief; a demonstrated result is harder to dispute and less likely to wound anyone's pride in the process. Showing tends to outlast telling.

Law 10

↑ Top

Infection: Avoid the Unhappy and Unlucky

Chronic misfortune or bitterness in someone close to you can be corrosive — not through any deliberate act, but through the way mood, luck, and reputation tend to rub off on the people around them. Greene's advice is to be deliberate about who you let into your circle for exactly this reason.

Law 11

↑ Top

Learn to Keep People Dependent on You

Being indispensable is a form of security. If the people above you rely on a skill, a relationship, or knowledge that only you hold, your position becomes harder to threaten or replace.

Law 12

↑ Top

Use Selective Honesty and Generosity to Disarm Your Victim

A single, well-timed act of genuine candor or kindness can lower someone's guard far more effectively than persistent flattery, precisely because it looks unstrategic. Greene notes this is precisely what makes it usable as a tool.

Law 13

↑ Top

When Asking for Help, Appeal to People's Self-Interest, Never to Their Mercy or Gratitude

Requests framed around what the other person stands to gain are received far better than requests that lean on sympathy or old debts, which can feel like a burden or an obligation rather than an opportunity.

Law 14

↑ Top

Pose as a Friend, Work as a Spy

Warmth and apparent interest are effective ways to get people to disclose what they'd otherwise guard closely. Understanding someone's private motivations and fears gives you a significant edge in predicting how they'll act.

Law 15

↑ Top

Crush Your Enemy Totally

A defeated but not fully neutralized rival can regroup and return with a grudge. The law argues for resolving conflicts decisively rather than leaving smoldering resentment that can reignite later.

Law 16

↑ Top

Use Absence to Increase Respect and Honor

Constant presence erodes its own value — familiarity breeds a kind of contempt, or at least indifference. Strategic withdrawal can restore the sense of scarcity that made you compelling in the first place.

Law 17

↑ Top

Keep Others in Suspended Terror: Cultivate an Air of Unpredictability

Predictable people are easy to plan around and, eventually, easy to outmaneuver. An element of genuine unpredictability keeps others cautious and reactive rather than confidently in control.

Law 18

↑ Top

Do Not Build Fortresses to Protect Yourself — Isolation Is Dangerous

Cutting yourself off from others in the name of safety also cuts you off from information, allies, and warning signs. Greene argues that real security comes from staying embedded in a network of relationships, not from retreating behind walls.

Law 19

↑ Top

Know Who You're Dealing With — Do Not Offend the Wrong Person

Not everyone responds to the same tactics the same way, and misjudging who you're up against — their temperament, their resources, their capacity for retaliation — can turn a minor move into a costly mistake.

Law 20

↑ Top

Do Not Commit to Anyone

Staying above permanent alliances preserves your independence and your ability to be courted by multiple sides. Once you're firmly identified with one camp, your value to everyone else declines.

Law 21

↑ Top

Play a Sucker to Catch a Sucker — Seem Dumber Than Your Mark

If people believe they're smarter than you, they let their guard down. Appearing less sharp or less threatening than you are can be a deliberate advantage rather than a liability.

Law 22

↑ Top

Use the Surrender Tactic: Transform Weakness into Power

Fighting a losing battle for the sake of pride tends to make outcomes worse. A tactical surrender buys time, avoids unnecessary damage, and can quietly shift the balance of power back in your favor later.

Law 23

↑ Top

Concentrate Your Forces

Spreading effort, attention, or resources too thin across many fronts dilutes their impact. Greene argues for committing intensely to your strongest opportunity rather than dividing yourself evenly.

Law 24

↑ Top

Play the Perfect Courtier

Every social or professional environment has unwritten rules of tact, deference, and self-presentation. Learning and observing them — without seeming to grovel — lets you navigate hierarchy without triggering resistance.

Law 25

↑ Top

Re-Create Yourself

Rather than accepting an identity handed to you by circumstance or other people's expectations, deliberately shaping how you present yourself gives you more control over how you're perceived and valued.

Law 26

↑ Top

Keep Your Hands Clean

Mistakes and unpleasant necessities are inevitable, but visibly owning every one of them damages your standing. Greene advises distancing yourself from the dirty work — using intermediaries where necessary — while your own image stays untarnished.

Law 27

↑ Top

Play on People's Need to Believe to Create a Cultlike Following

People have a deep appetite for belonging to something larger than themselves. Offering a compelling vision — kept a little vague and aspirational — can generate loyalty far beyond what a purely transactional relationship would produce.

Law 28

↑ Top

Enter Action with Boldness

Hesitation broadcasts doubt, and doubt invites challenge. Greene argues that decisive, confident action — even when it involves risk — earns more respect than cautious half-measures, and that boldness also tends to cover for small mistakes along the way.

Law 29

↑ Top

Plan All the Way to the End

Failing to think through the full consequences of an action — including how it ends, not just how it starts — leaves you exposed to being blindsided by developments you should have anticipated.

Law 30

↑ Top

Make Your Accomplishments Seem Effortless

Visible struggle undercuts the impression of mastery. When results look effortless, they inspire a kind of awe that hard-won, visibly laborious achievements rarely do.

Law 31

↑ Top

Control the Options: Get Others to Play with the Cards You Deal

Offering people a choice makes them feel empowered, but if every option available actually serves your interest, you retain control of the outcome while they retain only the feeling of having chosen freely.

Law 32

↑ Top

Play to People's Fantasies

Grim reality has limited appeal. Offering people an attractive story or vision — one that speaks to what they wish were true — tends to draw them in more powerfully than a sober, factual pitch.

Law 33

↑ Top

Discover Each Man's Thumbscrew

Everyone has an insecurity, a need, or a weak point. Identifying it — not necessarily to exploit it cruelly, but to understand what truly motivates someone — gives you real insight into how to work with or around them.

Law 34

↑ Top

Be Royal in Your Own Fashion: Act Like a King to Be Treated Like One

How you carry yourself shapes how others treat you. Projecting quiet self-assurance and dignity, rather than uncertainty or excessive humility, tends to be self-fulfilling.

Law 35

↑ Top

Master the Art of Timing

The same action can succeed or fail entirely based on when it's taken. Greene emphasizes patience — recognizing the right moment rather than forcing events before conditions favor you.

Law 36

↑ Top

Disdain Things You Cannot Have: Ignoring Them Is the Best Revenge

Reacting to a slight or an obstacle often gives it more power than it deserves, and can make a minor problem look more significant than it is. Sometimes deliberate indifference is the stronger move.

Law 37

↑ Top

Create Compelling Spectacles

Visual and symbolic gestures tend to leave a stronger impression than words alone. A striking, memorable display of intent or achievement communicates power more viscerally than an explanation would.

Law 38

↑ Top

Think as You Like but Behave Like Others

Openly displaying contempt for convention or for other people's values, even if you privately disagree with them, tends to alienate people and invite backlash. Conforming outwardly while keeping your own judgment private avoids unnecessary conflict.

Law 39

↑ Top

Stir Up Waters to Catch Fish

Calm, composed thinking gives you the advantage in any conflict; losing your temper hands the advantage to whoever provoked you. Greene also notes the reverse — that provoking an opponent into anger can be a deliberate tactic to make them less effective.

Law 40

↑ Top

Despise the Free Lunch

Things given away too easily are often valued less, and unconditional generosity can create suspicion rather than gratitude. Using money and favors deliberately — including sometimes paying full price rather than seeking discounts — can build a stronger reputation than constant frugality.

Law 41

↑ Top

Avoid Stepping into a Great Man's Shoes

Following directly after someone widely admired sets up an unfair comparison you're likely to lose. Greene advises carving out distinct territory and standards rather than trying to replicate a predecessor's legacy.

Law 42

↑ Top

Strike the Shepherd and the Sheep Will Scatter

Group resistance or unrest usually has an instigator behind it. Addressing that individual directly, rather than the group as a whole, tends to be a faster and more effective way to defuse the situation.

Law 43

↑ Top

Work on the Hearts and Minds of Others

Coercion breeds resentment and eventual resistance; genuine emotional buy-in creates lasting loyalty. Winning people over — appealing to what they care about — tends to be more durable than forcing compliance.

Law 44

↑ Top

Disarm and Infuriate with the Mirror Effect

Reflecting someone's own values, emotions, or behavior back at them can make them feel deeply understood — or, used differently, can unsettle them by showing them an unflattering version of themselves.

Law 45

↑ Top

Preach the Need for Change, but Never Reform Too Much at Once

People are naturally resistant to sweeping, abrupt change, even when they say they want reform. Framing new ideas in familiar, incremental terms — and pacing change carefully — avoids triggering a backlash.

Law 46

↑ Top

Never Appear Too Perfect

Visible flawlessness tends to generate envy rather than admiration. Acknowledging a minor weakness, crediting luck, or otherwise humanizing your success can defuse resentment before it builds.

Law 47

↑ Top

Do Not Go Past the Mark You Aimed For — In Victory, Learn When to Stop

Momentum after a win can tempt you into overreaching, chasing more than you originally set out to achieve. Greene warns that this is often when people make their costliest mistakes — success itself becomes the trap.

Law 48

↑ Top

Assume Formlessness

Rigid patterns and fixed positions are easy for others to study and counter. Staying adaptable and hard to predict — closer to water than stone, in Greene's framing — makes you far more difficult to pin down or defeat.

Note: these are original summaries of each law's core idea, not excerpts from the book. Greene's actual text develops each law with extended historical case studies, "reversals" (exceptions to the law), and more nuance than a one-paragraph summary can carry — worth going to the source for that.
