// feature guide

Everything Touch Grass does.

Touch Grass is a small ritual disguised as an app. Step away from the screen and go outside, walk for as long as you like, and come back to find a single strange thing waiting for you.

The whole experience lives inside one illustrated tarot card — a card, a button, a discovery at a time. With your permission it reads where you are, so the daylight, the weather, the moon's phase and the rare moments of the year are all true to your own sky. The painted card mirrors the world outside your window, and every find is tinted by the hour, the season and the conditions you walked through.

This page lays all of it out, feature by feature.

// how to use

  1. Open the app. A fresh invitation greets you — a quiet call to step out, different every time.
  2. Tap Head outside and go. The timer saves at once — close the tab, lock your screen, it doesn't matter.
  3. Walk for however long you like. Longer walks shift the odds toward rarer finds.
  4. Come back, open the app, tap Step back.
  5. See what you found. Tap Step inside when you're ready to go again — a fresh invitation will be waiting.
invitation

A fresh invitation, then a tagline

The home screen greets you with a short, ever-changing call to step outside — Slip Away, Into the Gold, Leave the Glow — another way of saying touch grass. It's written by Claude and chosen to suit the actual hour, so it won't chase the light at night or name the moon by day.

Beneath it sits a fresh tagline: dreamy, witty, a quiet play on leaving the screen behind, and ending on a soft full stop. A thin animated line sweeps across while the words load. To keep it from ever repeating itself, each visit quietly nudges the invitation and tagline down a different path, so no two arrivals feel quite the same.

With no API key, both the invitation and the tagline are drawn from built-in lists instead — no loading, no delay.

the threshold reading

A quiet reading above the call

In the sky above the invitation sits one faint reading — light enough never to pull focus from the call to go out. You choose which from the Display the… menu in the Keeper; it's remembered, and only ever shows one at a time.

  • Living World (the default) — the month and its nickname (June · Peak Daylight, Deepening Green) over a line of nature's call written fresh by the oracle, true to the season, hour, place and weather. Without a key it falls back to a hand-written seasonal note.
  • Tonight's Sky — an evocative "Tonight, …" line from the oracle, fresh each visit, over a handful of live readings: when the day's light turns to honey (or, once that's past, when Twilight's Edge begins); the moon's phase and whether it next rises or sets, and when; when the stars emerge — or, once it's already dark, when first light breaks; and the current zodiac sign with a countdown to its turning (In Cancer, the wheel turns in 30d).
  • Sun & Moon — the sun and the moon riding two arcs across the day, tucked to either edge. Each arc's height follows how high that body actually climbs, so a high summer sun draws a tall dome and a low winter sun a flat one, and the moon rides its own path — and each is captioned with the direction and time it rises and sets.
timer

Departure timer

Tap Head outside and the timer starts at once — your departure time is saved on your device the moment you tap.

Close the tab, lock your screen, restart the browser entirely. When you come back, the app picks up exactly where you left off and uses the elapsed time to decide what you found.

away

While you're out

A minimal screen shows your time out and your departure, each on its own quiet line: Wandering for 4m 32s and Set out at 17:23 (the app keeps to 24-hour time throughout). The elapsed time warms in colour as the walk grows:

  • Under 30 min — plain
  • 30 min – 1 hour — uncommon teal
  • 1 hour – 4 hours — rare amber
  • 4 hours and beyond — legendary purple

Close the tab at any point — when you return and tap Step back, the elapsed time is reckoned from your saved departure regardless.

finds

The finds

Every walk returns exactly one find — always. Never more, never less. Walk length shapes its rarity, not the quantity.

Each is conjured by Claude like an entry in a grimoire or the face of an unknown tarot card: surreal, esoteric, faintly macabre, with a thread of cosmic dread. It arrives as a single, complete sentence — short, and always finished, never cut off mid-thought — and it's coloured by the hour, season, weather, moon, the lie of the land around you, and any rare moment of the year you walked through.

Its subject roams widely from walk to walk — stone, water, a sound, a forgotten object, the occasional rare insect — and it deliberately avoids echoing your recent finds, so it never settles into one motif. On the result, the rarity wears a small sigil that grows with the tier: a seed for common, then four-, six- and eight-pointed stars. Four tiers of escalating strangeness:

  • common A small wrongness, a quiet omen — the world tilting half a degree.
  • uncommon An esoteric object or sign, clearly impossible, humming with hidden meaning.
  • rare A divinatory apparition — the veil thinning, something looking back.
  • legendary A cosmic, mythic revelation: vast, ancient, indifferent — the kind that rearranges you.

With no API key set, finds rise from a built-in pool — the "old book" — instead. If a key is set but the call fails, a find from the old book stands in and a quiet ⚠ the oracle was silent note appears in the bottom-right of the result.

divining

Divining your find

When you tap Step back, your rarity tier is rolled at once — before the find is written. The screen names the tier while the find is conjured, so you know what's coming before it arrives.

Uncommon, rare and legendary are colour-coded. Common simply says Divining your find… — no announcement for the everyday. A sweep line runs beneath while the words are in flight.

loop

The loop

The app moves in a deliberate circle. Tap Step inside on the result and you return to the home screen, where a fresh invitation loads. Tap Head outside when you're ready and the next loop begins. Nothing resets until you choose to start again.

scene

A living tarot card

The whole app lives inside an illustrated tarot card — a parchment frame edged with a fine double keyline, a full-bleed scene behind the words, and a title beneath each panel: The Threshold, The Wandering, The Omen, The Discovery, The Keeper. The art runs edge to edge; the text floats over a soft gradient scrim so it always stays readable.

The surface the card rests on is the ground you're about to walk out onto, drawn from where you really are: sand at the coast, meadow and loam in a forest, stone and brick in a city, slate on a mountain, open grass on a plain. Without a location it follows the season instead — a spring meadow, sun-baked summer earth, autumn ochre and heather, winter frost-stone. Its lightness is kept inverted against the card's interior, so a bright day rests on a deep ground and a cool night on a pale one and the card always reads. With a location on, it eases between them gradually as the sun crosses the horizon, rather than switching the moment day turns to dusk. A faint sheen drifts with the sun across the day, and a soft vignette frames the edges.

Along the bottom of the screen runs a very faint ticker — a single line, centred when it fits and never wrapping, each reading wearing its own small coloured glyph and divided by a thin rule. It carries where you are (coast), the current weather (with a windy note when it blows), the next turn of the day (sunset in 2h), and the days to the next full moon.

Most days it simply sits there; when it's wider than the screen it becomes a true ticker — drag it with a finger, or let it drift along on its own and bounce back. The lettering is a bright cream with a dark outline so it stays legible over any ground, bright midday or dim night.

masthead

The name & the creed

Above the card sit the app's name, Touch Grass, and its small creed — a small rite for going outside. The pair and the card are centred together, and the lettering quietly inverts with the day's surface — dark ink on the pale evening grounds, cream on the dark midday one.

Tucked into the top-right corner, a small open book opens this very guide in your browser.

sky

Sky, sun & moon

The sky is painted from the device clock and calendar — four moods (dawn, day, dusk, night) whose thresholds shift with the season, so a midsummer evening stays bright hours longer than a winter one.

A faced sun presides over the day, a faced moon over the night — each with a calm expression, teal eyes and rosy cheeks. The horizon never goes fully black: a warm glow and scattered city lights linger along it after dark.

stars

Your constellation overhead

After dusk, the constellation of the current zodiac sign rises in the corner — connect-the-dot stars following the real shape of each sign, glowing like small embers. Around it a faint field of background stars drifts and twinkles, reseeded every calendar day, so the night sky is never quite the same twice.

On a clouded night the background stars fade, but your own sign lingers faintly through the cloud — only fog, a storm, or falling rain or snow blot it out entirely. A bright moon washes some of them out too, exactly as it does in a real sky — the fainter background stars thin and dim as the moon waxes toward full, while your constellation stays put.

grass

Grass, flowers & small life

A band of grass, wildflowers, the odd bush and weed grows along the bottom, recoloured each season — fresh greens in spring, gold in summer, russet in autumn, muted in winter (no blooms then). The whole patch is regrown each calendar day, so its shapes and placement never sit the same way twice.

By day a few butterflies wander across; after dark, fireflies drift and pulse over the grass. And the season drifts through the air: tumbling leaves in autumn, blossom petals in spring.

sound

The Chorus — a soundscape you shape

An ambient bed plays across every screen, synthesized live rather than looped — there are no audio files at all. It's made for headphones and a long walk, and it's yours to shape: tap ♫ Tune the Chorus in the Keeper to open the mixer.

Start from one of six soundscapes, each a complete, hand-tuned scene:

  • Rain — a cozy, muffled downpour with a little wind, and distant thunder rolling under it.
  • Waves — slow ocean surf on a sea breeze, long swells rising and receding.
  • Stream — a bright woodland brook, steady and babbling, with birdsong over it.
  • Wind — open and windswept, a lone wind chime singing in the gusts.
  • Forest — a living wood: a leaf canopy over a hidden brook, rich with birds.
  • Snow — a muffled winter hush, a thin cold wind over a soft floor, almost still.

Refine any of them by hand — or build your own from silence — and save your blends as named mixes to return to. Beneath the presets sit the controls: seven nature layers you blend freely (rain, waves, stream, wind, a leaf-hush, a wind chime, and a warm floor under it all), and shapers for the whole — overall volume, brightness from dark to airy, and how much and how fast it drifts and swells.

Over that bed live a few voices, dialled by their own levels and an Activity knob for how often they speak up. The hour still shapes them: songbirds by day, crickets and an owl by night. Stay a while and rare omens drift past — a distant church bell, a cuckoo through spring and summer, a woodpecker by day, a wolf after dark, a shimmer when a star falls. Raise the rain and thunder answers; when rain and wind grow loud the birds hush beneath them, the way they really do.

Two quick toggles sit at the top of The Chorus: Stereo widens the beds into an enveloping field or narrows them to centre, and Voices silences all the living sounds for a pure bed. Everything flows — nothing cuts or jumps; each change glides gently in, and nothing ever repeats.

feedback

Taps & the reveal

Buttons answer with a soft tap. Stepping outside plays a gentle descending breath; returning to a discovery is met with a warm ascending shimmer — a small aha, distinct from every other sound. On phones, each is paired with a light haptic tick. The first tap on the page is what wakes the audio (a browser requirement).

motion

Respects reduced motion

If your device is set to reduce motion — or you turn Motion off in the Keeper — the drifting stars, fireflies, butterflies, falling snow, drifting clouds, rain and shooting stars all freeze in place. The scene stays, the movement stops.

place

Your real sky

With your permission, the app reads your location once — and only your rough coordinates, kept in your browser. From it, daylight becomes exact: the real sunrise, sunset and twilight where you are, so a midsummer dusk lingers as long as it truly does.

Deny it, or turn Place off in the Keeper, and everything falls back gracefully to seasonal estimates. Your rough coordinates are only ever sent to the open services that answer back — weather, elevation, and a coarse place-name lookup — and never stored anywhere but your own browser.

biome

A sense of where you are

From your rough location the app reads the lie of the land — coast, forest, city, mountain or open plain — and lets it colour the finds and the ground you'll walk out onto. The oracle leans toward salt and tideline by the sea, stone and thin air on a mountain, brick and the hum of streets in a city. It's kept deliberately coarse — a single word, never an address.

light

Golden hour & blue hour

The card mirrors not only the weather but the quality of the light. As the sun nears the horizon — at dawn and again at dusk — the sky warms through amber and rose, the sun deepens toward a sunset orange, and the grass catches the glow.

In the brief blue hour after sundown the sky sinks to deep indigo with a thin band of warmth left along the horizon. It eases in and out with the sun's true angle, never a hard switch, and softens under cloud. (Location on; otherwise the plain seasonal sky.)

weather

The weather outside, on the card

The scene mirrors the real sky. Clouds gather to the true cloud cover — a few drifting puffs, or a full grey overcast that veils the sun and thins the stars. Rain falls when it's raining, snow when it's snowing, never by guesswork. Fog rolls in as a soft, desaturating bank; thunderstorms darken the sky and flash. Wind speeds the clouds and stirs the air. (The soundscape is yours to set in The Chorus — it no longer follows the outside weather.)

moon

The true moon

The moon shows tonight's real phase. The faced disc stays whole and central — never a thin sliver — with a soft shadow drawn along the terminator across the unlit side. At full moon it's the bare, bright face; new and crescent nights wear more of the shadow, the face still watching through it. The ticker counts down the days to the next full moon.

moments

Rare moments through the year

On certain days the sky and the finds turn special — the solstices and equinoxes, the old cross-quarter festivals, the great meteor showers, the night of a full or new moon, the sun's passage into each zodiac sign, and more.

On those days the find and the home-screen invitation are coloured by the moment (its name glows in its own colour on the home screen), and on meteor-shower nights real shooting stars streak the clear sky.

weather now

What's outside, at a glance

When location is on, the ticker carries the current sky — a little drawn glyph, the weather in a single word (clear, a few clouds, fog, rain, snow, storms), the temperature, and a windy note when it's genuinely blustery. Just enough to know what you're stepping into — it's a walking app, not a weather app. Turn Place off and it isn't there.

countdown

The turning of the day

The ticker counts down to the next turn of the day — sunrise, noon, sunset or midnight, whichever comes next, from the real solar times where you are — a gentle sense of where the light is going.

And while you're out, when the evening golden hour is nearly upon you, a honey-coloured whisper appears beneath the timer — the light turns to honey in 18m — so you can be under it.

while you wander

A small rite for the walk

Beneath the timer, the card offers a single quiet rite to carry on your walk — "Find one thing older than you, and one thing younger." · "Let the next sound you can't name decide which way you turn."

They rotate slowly while you're out, each fading gently into the next, and every one points the same way: outward, into the world, away from the screen.

reliquary

The Reliquary — your kept finds

Every find is kept. Tap the small golden tarot-card mark tucked into the bottom-left grass (opposite the key) to open The Reliquary — a scrollable log titled What found you., newest first.

Each walk is a single compact line: its date, the time, and how long you were out. Tap any one to re-read it — the oracle's full words unfold, followed by a quiet note of its rarity and where it came to you (rare · found in the city during daytime).

The list rests below the sky and scrolls on its own as it fills. You can clear the most recent entry or the whole log, each behind a one-tap confirmation. It all lives in your browser, nowhere else.

setup

The Keeper

Tap the golden key in the bottom-right corner to open The Keeper (its companion, the card mark, sits opposite in the bottom-left).

Enter your Anthropic API key here — it stays in your browser and is only ever sent directly to Anthropic, when writing a find, a tagline, or a Threshold reading. You can also toggle Sound, Place (real location), Motion (freeze the scene) and Pings (notifications) on or off — each shows its state, lit for on and dim for off — open ♫ Tune the Chorus to shape the soundscape, and choose, from the Display the… menu, which reading sits on the Threshold.

Without a key, everything still works — finds and taglines rise from the old book instead.

the calls

Three quiet calls to go out

With your permission, the app can reach you in its own voice — never more than it must, each a single soft notification carrying the sun's face (the daytime sibling of the moon you launch it by). There are three:

  • The daily call — about two hours before sunset, a nudge to take today's walk while the light lasts. It holds its tongue if you've already been out that day, and steps aside whenever the golden-hour call is on offer.
  • Golden hour — roughly half an hour before the light turns to honey, a heads-up to step out and stand in it. It comes whether or not you've walked. (Location on — it's reckoned from the real sun.)
  • The almanac — on the special days (a meteor shower, a solstice, a full moon…), a word that the sky is marking the day. It comes whether or not you've walked, and may sit alongside the golden-hour call.

Each fires at most once a day, and they're on by default — your browser asks permission the first time. Switch them off any time with Pings in the Keeper.

Being a screen-free app with no server behind it, an installed copy can still be woken in the background to deliver them even while fully closed (on browsers that allow it). Otherwise they reach you when you next open the app near their hour. Either way, they won't chase you.

walk reminder

A thread back to the card

The moment you set out, a single standing reminder settles into the shade of your notifications — silent, in the app's voice, telling you the reading lies face-down until you return.

It lingers there for the whole walk (it won't buzz or auto-dismiss), and a tap reopens the app and turns you straight to the result — even if the system had closed it. Come back through the app and it lifts on its own.

persistence

Survives everything

The walk — your departure, the result, the screen you're on — is saved on your device and written automatically after every change. Close and reopen the app and it returns you to the exact panel you left, whether that's the Reliquary, the Keeper, a result, or a walk in progress with the timer still counting.

Your API key is kept separately, so it never resets when a new walk starts. Close the tab mid-walk, mid-result, mid-anything — the app picks up exactly where you left off.

// the app is complete

That's all of it. Touch Grass is finished — every screen, every sound, every quiet detail above is in your hands now. There's no next version waiting in the wings; the most fitting thing left to do is close the tab and go outside.

// appendix · preview parameters

For the curious and the tinkerers: add any of these to the address bar to summon a condition without waiting for the world to provide it. They're grouped below by what they touch, and combine freely — for instance ?tod=dusk&light=golden, or ?biome=coast&wx=fog.

Time & season

  • ?tod=dawn|day|dusk|night — force the time of day
  • ?season=spring|summer|autumn|winter — force the season

Sky, light & weather

  • ?light=golden|blue|day|night — force the golden or blue hour
  • ?wx=clear|cloudy|overcast|fog|rain|snow|thunder — force the weather
  • ?moon=0–1 — force the moon's phase (0 new · 0.5 full · 1 new)

Place & ground

  • ?biome=coast|forest|city|mountain|plain — force the sense of place
  • ?surface=0–8 — force a fixed ground surface (normally the ground follows your biome, or the season without a location)

Moments & calls

  • ?event=<key> — force an almanac moment, e.g. ?event=perseids
  • ?call=now|daily|golden|almanac — fire one of the call notifications straight away
