The Journal of Delights

A short field guide to the app and the small daily habit it’s built around — based on Ross Gay’s The Book of Delights.

01What this is

One delight a day, noticed closely and written down — somewhere between a sentence and a short essay. The app is a friendlier second doorway to a Notion database where the entries actually live; you can always open the same data in Notion itself.

It is not a gratitude journal, and deliberately so. There’s no scoring, no mood tracking, no rating, no streak counter. Those add an evaluative layer that quietly turns noticing into performance — which is the opposite of the point.

02What a delight is — lightly held

Take this as orientation, not instruction. The practice is personal; these are just the contours Gay’s book sketches.

  • It’s an act of attention, not an event. Usually small and ordinary — a stranger’s gesture, an overheard phrase, the shape of light on a wall. The discipline is in the noticing, not in finding something impressive.
  • It doesn’t require a good day. Delight sits comfortably beside grief, worry, and difficulty rather than denying them. A hard day can still hold one. A missed day is not a failure — just a day.
  • It resists categories. Tags and people emerge from what you actually wrote, not from a list decided in advance. Let them accrue their own meaning over time.
  • One a day is the whole shape of it. Even on a day full of delights, choosing one and writing it is what builds the practice. That’s why the app keeps to a single entry per date.

None of this is a rule the app enforces on your feelings. Write what you want to keep. The only real constraint is one entry per calendar day.

03Using the app

Writing today’s delight

Tap Today in the slim bar. If you haven’t written for today it opens a fresh entry with the cursor already in the writing; if you have, it opens the one you wrote so you can read or edit it — never a second one. ⌘/Ctrl + Enter saves from anywhere in the form. Whatever you type is kept as a local draft as you go, so a dropped connection or a closed tab never loses a delight.

Interrupted? Save a draft

If you can’t finish right now, hit Save draft — the delight is kept on your device and waits for you at the top of the List as an unfinished row. Tap it to resume exactly where you left off, or discard it. (Drafts autosave as you write too; Save draft just makes it deliberate. Cancel throws the in-progress entry away.)

Works offline

Your delights live in Notion, but the app keeps a local copy of your last synced entries, so you can read your whole journal offline. Write while you’re offline too: the delight saves on your device with a small unsynced marker and syncs itself to Notion the moment you’re back online — a tunnel, a plane, or a dead zone never blocks a day. Install it (below) and it opens offline as well.

The fields

  • Title — a few words for the delight’s essence. Optional, but it makes the list easier to wander.
  • Date — defaults to today; you can write into the past. Past entries stay editable indefinitely.
  • Entry — the writing itself. A live word count sits below it as gentle company, never a gate — shorter and longer both save fine.
  • Tags & People — freeform. Start typing to reuse one you’ve used before, or create a new one inline. People are kept separate from Tags so “everything mentioning so-and-so” stays clean.
  • Word Count — computed by Notion automatically after each save, so it’s shown read-only on a saved entry.
  • Photo — optional, at most one per delight, added from the entry editor (even on a past entry) with the option to replace or remove it. It's resized down before upload — the feeling matters more than the resolution. It shows as a small faded, grainy, slightly tilted thumbnail in the list (tap it to see it full size) and, uncropped, at its own size on the entry itself.

Three ways to read

The slim bar switches between List (newest first, with excerpts), Calendar (a month grid; days with an entry carry a gold dot), and Heatmap (the whole year at a glance — each lit square a day you wrote). In Calendar and Heatmap, tapping a written day opens it and tapping an empty day starts one. The app always opens in List; Calendar and Heatmap are a tap away in the view selector.

Finding & revisiting

  • Search — the bar’s search box, with a scope menu to look across all, just the text, just tags, or just people.
  • Tap a chip — clicking any tag or person on an entry filters the list down to it.
  • On this day — when an entry shares its calendar day with delights from other years, an optional On this day button appears; it only shows the past when you ask it to.
  • Stats — a quiet portrait of the practice: totals, recent cadence, word measures, and your most-noticed tags and people. Descriptive only — no streaks, no scores.

Export

The export button in the bar saves your whole journal as a single self-contained HTML file — styling and icons baked in, no internet needed to read it later. Your words are always yours to keep.

A Light/Dark toggle in the bar switches between two palettes (Solarized) — dark by default, and your choice is remembered. This guide follows the same choice (with its own toggle top-right). On a phone the whole bar stays one row: search and Today sit in reach, while the views collapse into a small menu and Stats, Export, the theme toggle, this guide and Settings into a ⋯ menu.

Install it

In Chrome or Edge (desktop or Android, including a Samsung Galaxy), use Install app from the address bar or menu to add it to your home screen. It runs full-screen like a native app, opens offline, and syncs your writing to Notion whenever it has a connection.

Gentle reminders

With your permission, the app can nudge you in its own quiet voice — never more than two a day. There are two:

  • The evening nudge — around 9pm, if today doesn’t have a delight written yet. It holds its tongue the moment you’ve written one.
  • On this day — around 7pm, a short note when a past year shares today’s calendar day. If nothing from years past falls on today, nothing shows at all.

Turn them on from ⚙ Settings; each can be switched off on its own. They’re local and best-effort — nothing is sent on your behalf, delivery while the app is fully closed only works in an installed app on Android or desktop Chrome, and timing is approximate rather than exact.

04Make it your own journal

Anyone can run their own Journal of Delights against their own Notion — entirely separate from anyone else’s. Until you connect one, the app stays in demo mode on sample delights stored only on your device. To keep real entries you need two things: a Notion database with the right columns, and an integration token that lets the app read and write it. Setting this up takes a few minutes and needs no coding.

Option A — duplicate the ready-made Starter Template (easiest)

Duplicate the Journal of Delights Starter Template — an empty database with the exact columns and the Word Count formula already set up. Open it and use Duplicate (top-right ••• → Duplicate) to make your own private copy — the columns and formula come along automatically — then jump to step 05 to connect it.

Option B — build an empty one

In Notion, create a new page and add a Table database (full-page is tidiest). Name it Journal of Delights. Then set up exactly these columns — the names matter, because the app looks them up by name:

ColumnTypeWhat it holds
TitleTitleThe default first column. A short name for the delight, e.g. the espresso foam.
DateDateThe day of the entry. One entry per day.
EntryTextThe writing itself. Keep it as a Text property (a column), not the page body.
TagsMulti-selectLeave the options empty — they fill in as you write.
PeopleMulti-selectAlso start empty. Kept separate from Tags on purpose.
Word CountFormulaCounts words automatically. Paste the formula below.
PhotoFiles & mediaOptional. Leave empty — the app uploads into it. At most one file is ever used.

For the Word Count column, choose type Formula, click Edit formula, and paste:

length(prop("Entry")) - length(replaceAll(prop("Entry"), " ", "")) + 1

That’s every column. You can delete any extra columns Notion added by default (like a stray “Tags” it pre-creates) as long as the seven above exist with these exact names. The app never writes to Word Count — Notion keeps it up to date by itself.

05Create the integration & connect

An internal integration is just a key that lets this one app reach your database. Here’s the whole path, step by step:

  1. Create the integration. Go to notion.so/my-integrations and click New integration. Give it a name (e.g. Journal of Delights), pick the workspace your database lives in, and leave the type as Internal. Save it.
  2. Copy the token. On the integration’s page, open Configuration (or Secrets) and find Internal Integration Secret. Click Show, then Copy. It starts with ntn_ (older ones start with secret_). Treat it like a password.
  3. Share the database with it. Open your Journal of Delights database in Notion. Top-right ••• menu → Connections (some workspaces label it Add connections) → search your integration’s name → select it. This is what grants read/write access — without it the app sees nothing.
  4. Get the database link. Still on the database, ••• → Copy link (or just copy the URL from your browser’s address bar while viewing it).
  5. Connect in the app. Open the app, tap ⚙ settings in the top bar, paste the token into the first field and the database link into the second, and Save. The app flips from demo to your live journal immediately.

Both the token and the database link are stored only in this browser and relayed to Notion through this site’s proxy — never saved on a server. Anyone using this browser profile can reach your journal, so disconnect from settings (which clears the token) on shared devices. To switch journals later, just paste a different database link.

06Chosen, not missing

The app reads, creates, and edits — and that is deliberately all. There’s no delete: entries are never destroyed from here (remove one in Notion if you truly must), because a delight, once noticed, is worth keeping. There are no moods, no ratings, no streaks — nothing that turns noticing into a score. What can look like something missing is the gain: each absence is a deliberate choice that keeps this a small, calm tool, asking only for your attention. This isn’t an early version with more bolted on later — it’s the finished shape, and this guide won’t grow past it.
