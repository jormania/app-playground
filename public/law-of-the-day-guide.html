Law of the Day

A daily quiz built on Robert Greene's The 48 Laws of Power.

Law of the Day is a small, unofficial fan project inspired by The 48 Laws of Power. It isn't affiliated with Robert Greene or his publisher — it's just a one-minute daily habit of reading a short scenario, guessing which law it illustrates, and seeing the explanation.

How it works

  • One scenario a day. Read the short situation on the home screen — a contemporary, relatable moment where one of the 48 laws is quietly at play.
  • Pick a law. Four options are shown, one of which is correct. Tapping an option is the answer — there's no separate submit step.
  • See the reveal. You'll immediately see whether you were right, the correct law's name, and a short explanation of why the scenario demonstrates it.
  • One attempt per day. Once you've answered, the day locks — your result stays visible until a new scenario unlocks at local midnight.

Difficulty

Three tiers, set by the meter icon

Tap the meter icon next to the title to cycle through three levels. Difficulty is never about more options or a timer — it only changes how close the three wrong answers sit to the right one, so a harder tier means every option is a more tempting trap:

  • Standard. The wrong answers are a fair spread — usually easy to narrow down once you know the law.
  • Complex. Every wrong answer is drawn from the same strategic family as the right one (laws about concealment, reputation, force, and so on), so there are no throwaway options and the mix keeps changing.
  • Extreme. The wrong answers are the very nearest cousins of the correct law — and the app leans on the ones you personally tend to confuse, based on your own history — so each choice genuinely could be right.

Your choice sticks between days, and changing it before you answer reshuffles today's options on the spot.

Rotation

How the daily law is chosen

All 48 laws are shuffled once into a "season." Each day draws the next law from that shuffled order, so no law repeats until every other law in the season has appeared. Once all 48 have been shown, the app reshuffles into a new season and continues — spaced repetition, not pure randomness, so you won't see the same law twice in a short stretch.

Streak

What the streak counts

The streak counts consecutive days answered, not consecutive correct answers — showing up and taking a guess keeps it alive, even on the days you get it wrong. Missing a day resets it back to one the next time you answer.

Stats

Tracking your progress

Tap the chart icon next to the title to see your stats: your current streak and your best streak ever, how many scenarios you've answered in total, how many full seasons you've completed, how many of the 48 laws you've seen so far, your overall accuracy, and a per-law breakdown of every law you've answered. Scenarios answered can climb past 48 once you've been through more than one season — it's a running total, while laws seen is capped at the 48 you can ever have. A season is one full shuffled cycle through all 48 laws, so seasons completed grows every 48 answers. It's all computed from the same on-device history described below — nothing new is collected, it's just a view onto what's already there.

Privacy

Where your data lives

Everything — your current season, streak, and today's result — is stored only in this device's local storage. There's no account, no sync across devices, and no server involved. Clearing your browser's site data for this app will reset your progress.

Reference

All 48 laws, in one place

Want to browse beyond the daily scenario? See the full list of all 48 laws, each with a short explanation — handy as a refresher or a way to preview what's still ahead in your current season.

A gentle word

The point isn't to master 48 rules of manipulation — it's the minute of thinking each scenario invites: noticing the strategic dynamic playing out in an ordinary moment, right or wrong. This is an unofficial fan project inspired by Robert Greene's The 48 Laws of Power, made for that purpose alone — not affiliated with the author or publisher, and not a substitute for reading the book itself.
